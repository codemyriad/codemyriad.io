A toy for re-creating the Droste effect from Escher's *Print Gallery*.

Inspired by M. C. Escher's lithograph *Prentententoonstelling* (1956) — Dutch for *print exhibition*. The name **tententoon** is a playful contraction of that title.

Escher left a famous white blot at the centre of the print: the spiral was so tight that he couldn't draw it any further. In 2003, Hendrik Lenstra and Bart de Smit worked out the underlying math — a conformal map built from **complex exponentials and logarithms** — and completed the picture by computer.

In 2026, **Grant Sanderson** (of *3Blue1Brown*) published a beautifully animated video walking through the paper. Highly recommended — it is the clearest tour of the math behind this toy.

This editor is a small interactive take on that idea: pick a rectangle inside any image and watch it spiral into itself forever. *(Complex numbers are fun. So are logarithms.)*

Tententoon sits at a different register from the other projects on this page. It is not a client engagement, not an open-source product with users to support, not infrastructure-in-waiting. It is a small toy Silvio built around something he wanted to play with — the math behind Escher's *Print Gallery*, and the question of what that math feels like as something to interact with. It is the kind of fun he often has around math and educational ideas, usually with his son in mind.

Listing it here is deliberate. We work on serious tools for real businesses, but we also build things because the underlying ideas are interesting to us. The same curiosity that pulled Silvio toward local-first patterns, or toward vetting the off-the-shelf software for a real bookshop, is the curiosity that produced this. Sometimes that curiosity produces a transcription backend; sometimes it produces a Droste-effect editor. It is the same instinct at different scales.
